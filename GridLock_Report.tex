\begin{abstract}
    On-street illegal parking and spillover parking near commercial areas, metro stations, and events severely choke carriageways and intersections in major metropolitan areas like Bengaluru. Currently, enforcement remains patrol-based and reactive, lacking dynamic heatmaps to correlate parking violations with congestion impact, making targeted enforcement difficult. This report details \textbf{GridLock-R2}, an AI-driven parking intelligence system developed to detect illegal parking hotspots, quantify their marginal impact on traffic flow, and mathematically optimize targeted enforcement operations. 
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction and Operational Challenge}

Urban mobility is heavily hindered by unauthorized parking. Vehicles parked illegally on main roads, footpaths, and near transit hubs significantly reduce the effective width of the carriageway, forming critical bottlenecks. 

\subsection{Why It’s Hard Today}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{Enforcement is Reactive:} Current traffic police operations rely on localized patrol intuition or ad-hoc complaints rather than data-driven deployment.
    \item \textbf{No Correlation with Congestion Impact:} There is no existing heatmap that maps parking violation volume directly to network delay or congestion risk.
    \item \textbf{Difficult to Prioritize Zones:} Without impact quantification, it is impossible for dispatchers to distinguish between a high-volume parking hotspot on a quiet residential street versus a low-volume hotspot that bottlenecks a critical arterial road.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Problem Statement}
\textit{How can AI-driven parking intelligence detect illegal parking hotspots and quantify their impact on traffic flow to enable targeted enforcement?}

This submission provides the necessary implementation, codebase, and documentation addressing the problem statement by bridging the gap between raw violation data, congestion metrics, and resource allocation.

\section{System Architecture: GridLock-R2}

GridLock-R2 addresses these challenges through a three-stage pipeline:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Bayesian Data Calibration:} Filtering unreliable and biased enforcement data.
    \item \textbf{AI Hotspot Prediction (GLM) \& Impact Quantification:} Forecasting future hotspots and measuring their congestion cost.
    \item \textbf{Operations Research (Greedy Knapsack):} Optimizing patrol deployments based on quantified impact.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Stage 1: Bayesian Validation and Data Cleaning}
Raw traffic violation data suffers from severe officer-level selection bias. Some officers mass-upload images from cameras, while others log specific localized violations, distorting the spatial distribution of actual violations.

To solve this, GridLock utilizes a Bayesian Beta-Binomial model to estimate the true validity rate ($\hat{p}$) of each officer's records. Records from low-confidence officers ($\hat{p} < 0.50$) are programmatically excluded from the spatial prediction pipeline. This ensures the model learns true geographic hotspots rather than the patrolling habits of specific officers.

\subsection{Stage 2: AI Hotspot Prediction and Impact Quantification}
We aggregated the cleaned Bengaluru traffic data into a spatial hex-grid (Uber H3, Resolution 9) at a monthly level.

\subsubsection{Autoregressive Poisson GLM}
We predict future violations using a Poisson Generalized Linear Model (GLM). The model uses historical autoregressive lags (e.g., previous month's volume) and a \textit{Persistence Index} to predict the next month's violation counts. 

\subsubsection{Quantifying Traffic Flow Impact (The BPR Model)}
To answer the core problem statement—\textit{quantifying impact on traffic flow}—we integrated OpenStreetMap (OSM) metadata with the Bureau of Public Roads (BPR) delay function. 

For each identified hotspot, we calculate the Marginal Delay Averted per vehicle cleared:
\begin{equation}
    t = t_0 \left( 1 + \alpha \left( \frac{V}{C - C_{parked}} \right)^\beta \right)
\end{equation}

By dynamically identifying the road type (e.g., Arterial, Residential) and calculating the capacity reduction ($C_{parked}$) caused by illegal parking, we convert parking violations into \textbf{Seconds of Delay per Vehicle}. This directly quantifies the congestion impact of each parking hotspot.

\subsection{Stage 3: Targeted Enforcement via Greedy Knapsack}
GridLock-R2 translates predictions into action using a Resource Simulator based on a Greedy Knapsack algorithm. Given a constrained budget of Tow Trucks and Patrol Cars, the system allocates resources to maximize the total network savings (delay averted). 

Tow trucks are directed to high-delay, high-severity nodes, while patrol officers (who possess a higher hourly clearance rate of 2 vehicles/hr, empirically derived from historical dataset averages) are deployed to maximize geographic coverage of volume hotspots.

\section{Evaluation and Results}

GridLock-R2 was rigorously evaluated using Walk-Forward Cross-Validation across five months (Nov 2023 - Apr 2024).

\subsection{Statistical Reliability}
Fold 1 is excluded throughout because the lag feature is undefined with one training month. Across folds with sufficient training history, our zone-ranking correlates with actual next-month violations at $\rho \approx 0.78$. We have not yet shown this beats a simple persistence baseline on top-K volume capture — the ensemble's value is that it never underperforms that baseline, while ranking quality genuinely improves.

\subsection{Operational Lift}
When evaluating the holdout test set (April 2024), the Volume-Only Poisson GLM yields a \textbf{+17.0\% lift at K=20} over a naive persistence baseline in identifying valid, high-impact hotspots.
